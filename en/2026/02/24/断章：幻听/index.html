Fragment: Auditory Hallucinations

2026-02-24 · 675/2min

Views:

When a fridge is running and you open the door, there's a faint hum.

Have you heard it?

It's light — almost weightless — as if another world exists inside the fridge. You open the door, and for a brief moment, two worlds meet. The cold compartment usually has a warm yellow light. Old fridges still use freon; the compressor makes its own little noises.

I think it sounds like a portal through time and space. Open the door, and from somewhere impossibly far away — from the other side of time and distance — comes a sound like the halo of an angel.

It's abstract, this sound.

But I believe most people have heard it. Most people can imagine it.

What if it never stopped?

Today I sat at my computer. Behind me, the range hood droned. Everything was vibrating — fans asserting their existence — and I tried to use my laptop.

Then —

I heard it again. That sound, as if from another world, calling to me with everything it had.

I once saw a stone wall at the Sanqing Taoist Temple in Qingdao. Legend says that those who have cultivated true goodness can pass straight through the wall. So many monks went and threw themselves against it. As a child, I thought: if it's just about passing through, then only the soul crosses over while the body stays behind — wouldn't that count as a kind of perfection too?

The senses are usually lying to you.

In that moment, my laptop screen felt like a wall, and those rustling, whispering sounds were reaching through time and space, beckoning me.

If I threw myself headfirst into the screen, would anything be different?

It took me five days to realise these were auditory hallucinations.

A false sensation.

I turned the volume up, then down. Windows played its calibration chime: ding-dong — that one was real.

The clock ticks once every second.

There's a metric called HRV that quantifies stress. Many smartwatches can track it. My stress index runs red for most of the day — asleep or awake — and I don't know whether I'm walking the edge of losing my mind entirely. Meanwhile the screen — the fridge — the desk lamp — the outside world.

It keeps humming.

In middle school and high school, I believed a cricket lived inside my desk lamp.

I tried turning the lamp on and off. The current flowed through; I tried to electrocute the cricket. I tried many times. I always failed.

The cricket's sound never went away.

So I lived in a kind of reverence too — for the life that had taken up residence inside my lamp.

All of this sounds like sleep-talk. Or maybe a hallucination.

The entire galaxy is collapsing inward, with me as its distant axis.

Then it all converges into a new singularity,

and I am waiting for the Big Bang.
